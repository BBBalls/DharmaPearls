The Medium Discourses

Chapter 7: King Dīrghāyu

This chapter of the Medium Discourses contains 15 sutras, beginning with its namesake, a lengthy sutra in which the Legend of King Dīrghāyu is embedded. Eight sutras have parallels in the Majjhima Nikāya, six in the Aṅguttara Nikāya, and one doesn't appear to have a parallel.

English translations currently available with brief synopses of their contents are listed below. Pali equivalents are listed in () and the sourcetext is indicated in [].

72. The Legend of King Dīrghāyu (MN 128: Corruptions) [T 26.532c10]

The Buddha visits the monks in Kauśāmbī to find them quarrelling with each other constantly. He tells them the legend of King Dīrghāyu, a story about how a feud between two kings was ended, in an attempt to convince them to stop. Failing that, he leaves and travels alone. He meets with Venerable Bhagu, who was practicing alone in a temple grove. After a brief conversation, the Buddha goes to another grove to spend some time alone. There he meets an elephant who happens by after leaving his herd for some time alone. Finally, the Buddha travels to a grove where Venerable Aniruddha, Nandika, and Kimbila are living together and practicing meditation. He gives them a lengthy discourse on 11 things that impede deep concentration.

73. The Gods (AN 8.64: At Gāyā Head) [T 26.539b20]

Picking up where the Legend of King Dīrghāyu left off, the Buddha discusses his examination of the heavens using the knowledge gained in meditation. The production of light and seeing forms refers to using the divine eye to see the heavens.

74. Eight Thoughts (AN 8.30: Anuruddha and the Great Thoughts) [T 26.540c19]

Aniruddha has seven thoughts about how the path is obtained, and the Buddha visits him about it. Praising him, the Buddha suggests adding an eighth thought, and later he gives a discourse on the subject. Aniruddha becomes an arhat shortly thereafter.

75. The Pure Way to the Imperturbable (MN 106: Conducive to the Imperturbable) [T 26.542b4]

The Buddha explains the different mental orientations that lead to rebirth in the formless heavens, and how they differ from the orientation that leads to Nirvāṇa.
