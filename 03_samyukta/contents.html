The Related Discourses

(Skt. Saṃyukta Āgama; Ch. 雜阿含經)

The Related Disources was translated to Chinese by Guṇabhadra and Baoyun sometime between 435-443 CE. The text as it has survived in the Taisho Daizokyo consists of 1,362 individually numbered sutras, although the numbering of abbreviated sutras is inconsistent (i.e., if they are all counted, the number is larger). The text spans 50 fascicles and 373 pages of Taisho volume 2.

The Pali equivalent is the Saṃyutta Nikāya (SN), and the two collections do overlap extensively. Unlike its Pali counterpart, however, the Related Discourses lacks explicit subdivisions. Indeed, the original Chinese text lacked subdivisions, sutra titles, or even numbers. Nonetheless, careful reading and comparisons to the Pali show that it did consist of thematically arranged groups of sutras, similar to SN but in a different arrangement.

Below is a list of the English translations currently available with brief synopses of their contents. Pali equivalents are listed in () and the sourcetext is indicated in [].

Note: All titles for SA sutras are supplied by the translator as an aid for the reader. They are not in the original Chinese. The sutras are numbered according to the Taisho, adding letters (e.g. 800a) when numbering abbreviated sutras the Taisho skipped.

The Path (cf. SN 45: Maggasaṃyutta)

SA 748: The First Sign (SN 45.55: Accomplishment in Proper Attention (1st) ) [T 99.198b5]

Right view is the first sign of liberation like the first light of dawn is the first sign of sunrise.

SA 749: Forerunners (SN 45.1: Ignorance) [T 99.198b14]

When producing insight is the forerunner, good things, conscience, right view, and the eightfold path lead to liberation.

SA 750: The Roots of Good and Evil [T 99.198b26]

The root of all evil is ignorance, and the root of all good is insight.

SA 751: The Right Undertaking (SN 45.24: Practice (2nd)) [T 99.198c14]

Following the eightfold path is the correct undertaking for lay and renunciant practitioners.

SA 752: Desire (SN 45.30: With Uttiya) [T 99.198c27]

Desire is not the object but the craving of desiring things. The eightfold path is the way to ending it.

SA 753: Deathlessness (SN 45.7: A Mendicant (2nd)) [T 99.199a13]

The Buddha explains that the deathless in his teaching is the end of the contaminants that lead to rebirth.

SA 754: Requirements of Concentration [T 99.199a22]

The Buddha explains that the first seven parts of the eightfold path are the root and requirements of right concentration.

SA 758: The Fear of Losing Mother or Son (AN 3.62: Perils ) [T 99.199b5]

The Buddha describes three common fears of losing one’s mother or son during disasters, but he says those fears really come from sickness, old age, and death. The way to escaping these fears is the eightfold path.

SA 759: Three Sensations (SN 45.29: Feeling) [T 99.199c17]

The eightfold path is the way to end the three sensations.

SA 760: Three Disagreeable Things (AN 10.76: Three Things) [T 99.199c27]

The eightfold path is the way to end old age, illness, and death.

SA 761: Learners and Non-Learners (SN 45.13: A Trainee) [T 99.200a14]

The terms "learner" and "non-learner" refer to disciples who are learning or have finished learning the eightfold path.
