The Related Discourses

1. The Aggregates (cf. SN 22: Khandhasaṃyutta)

This group of sutras has the common theme of describing the five aggregates and how they relate to Buddhist practice. These aggregates are form, feeling, perception, intention, and consciousness. The five aggregates represent the things that people can identify with as a self, and they are the basic constituents of everyday experience. When they come together, they form sentient beings, which in Buddhist thought includes animals, humans, and other beings like spirits, demons, and gods.

The Aggregates group consists of 110 full sutras plus 68 abbreviated sutra variants for a total of 178 sutras. Below is a list of the English translations currently available with brief synopses of their contents.

Pali equivalents are listed in () and the sourcetext is indicated in [].

Note: All titles for SA sutras are supplied by the translator as an aid for the reader. In this group, the sutra titles are based on the summary verses as much as possible.

1. Impermanent (SN 22.12, 22.51) [T 99.1a6]

When rightly contemplated, the aggregates are recognized to be impermanent. That contemplation leads to disillusionment and dispassion, which then lead to liberation.

2. Painful (SN 22.13, 22.51) [T 99.1a14]

When rightly contemplated, the aggregates are recognized to be painful. That contemplation leads to disillusionment and dispassion, which then lead to liberation.

3. Empty (SN 22.51:The End of Relishing) [T 99.1a14]

When rightly contemplated, the aggregates are recognized to be empty. That contemplation leads to disillusionment and dispassion, which then lead to liberation.

4. Not Self (SN 22.14, 22.51) [T 99.1a14]

When rightly contemplated, the aggregates are recognized to be not self. That contemplation leads to disillusionment and dispassion, which then lead to liberation.

5. Rightly Considered (SN 22.52: End of Relishing (2)) [T 99.1a15]

When the aggregates are properly considered and known as they really are to be impermanent, the practitioner can abandon desire and greed for them. Doing so leads to liberation.

6. Not Knowing (SN 22.24: Directly Knowing) [T 99.1a28]

Knowing and abandoning desire for the aggregates, one is able to end suffering.

7. Not Knowing (2) (SN 22.24: Directly Knowing) [T 99.1b6]

Knowing and abandoning desire for the aggregates, one’s mind can be liberated and transcend the fear of birth, old age, illness, and death.

8. Not Knowing (3) (SN 22.24 Directly Knowing) [T 99.1b16]

Knowing and abandoning desire for the aggregates, one’s mind can be liberated and end suffering.

9. Not Knowing (4) (SN 22.24 Directly Knowing) [T 99.1c2]

Knowing and abandoning desire for the aggregates, one’s mind can be liberated and transcend the fear of birth, old age, illness, and death.

10. Delighting in Form (SN 22.29: Taking Pleasure) [T 99.1c11]

Delighting in the aggregates is the same as delighting in suffering, which makes liberation from it impossible.
