The Related Discourses

13. The Factors of the Noble Path (cf. SN 45: Maggasaṃyutta)

This group of sutras has the common theme of describing the eight factors of the noble path, which in English is commonly called the eightfold path. These factors are right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right concentration. These eight factors serve as a guide to the Buddhist practice, and the eightfold path is the definition of the fourth noble truth.

This group consists of 46 full sutras plus 68 abbreviated sutra variants. Below is a list of the English translations currently available with brief synopses of their contents. Pali equivalents are listed in () and the sourcetext is indicated in [].

Note: All titles for SA sutras are supplied by the translator as an aid for the reader.

1. The First Sign (SN 45.55: Accomplishment in Proper Attention (1st) ) [T 99.198b5]

Right view is the first sign of liberation like the first light of dawn is the first sign of sunrise.

2. Forerunners (SN 45.1: Ignorance) [T 99.198b14]

When producing insight is the forerunner, good things, conscience, right view, and the eightfold path lead to liberation.

3. The Roots of Good and Evil [T 99.198b26]

The root of all evil is ignorance, and the root of all good is insight.

4. The Right Task (SN 45.24: Practice (2nd)) [T 99.198c14]

Following the eightfold path is the correct task for lay and renunciant practitioners.

5. Desire (SN 45.30: With Uttiya) [T 99.198c27]

Desire is not the thing desired but the craving of it. The eightfold path is the way to ending this problem.

6. Deathlessness (SN 45.7: A Mendicant (2nd)) [T 99.199a13]

The Buddha explains that the deathless in his teaching is the end of the contaminants that lead to rebirth.

7. Requirements (SN 45.28: Immersion) [T 99.199a22]

The Buddha explains that the first seven parts of the eightfold path are the roots and requirements of right concentration.

8. Desire (2) (SN 45.30: With Uttiya) [T 99.199b03]

Desire is not the thing desired but the craving of it. The eightfold path is the way to ending this problem.

9. Deathlessness (2) (SN 45.7: A Mendicant (2nd)) [T 99.199b03]

The Buddha explains that the deathless in his teaching is the end of the contaminants that lead to rebirth.

10. Requirements (2) (SN 45.28: Immersion) [T 99.199b03]

The Buddha explains that the first seven parts of the eightfold path are the roots and requirements of right concentration.

11. Fear of Losing Mother and Son (AN 3.62: Perils ) [T 99.199b5]

The Buddha describes three common fears of losing one’s mother or son during disasters, but he says those fears really come from sickness, old age, and death. The way to escaping these fears is the eightfold path.

12. Sensation (SN 45.29: Feeling) [T 99.199c17]

The eightfold path is the way to end the three sensations.

13. Disagreeable Things (AN 10.76: Three Things) [T 99.199c27]

The eightfold path is the way to end old age, illness, and death.

14. Trainees and Non-Trainees (SN 45.13: A Trainee) [T 99.200a14]

The terms “trainee” and “non-trainee” refer to disciples who are learning or have finished learning the eightfold path.

15. Correct Men and Great Men [T 99.200a22]

The terms “correct man” and “great man” refer to disciples who are learning or have finished learning the eightfold path.

16. The Noble End of Contaminants [T 99.200a23]

The contaminants are ended by accomplishing the eightfold path.

16. The Factors of the Noble Path [T 99.200a28]

The noble path consists of eight factors.
